# Idea Selection

Use this note when the user is deciding whether a research direction is worth serious investment.

These heuristics are synthesized from Nicholas Carlini's March 9, 2026 essay on high-impact research and adapted into a task-first skill workflow. They are judgment aids, not guarantees.

## Core questions

- If everything works, does the best-case conclusion say something important, or only that a metric moved a little?
- Is the problem important because it changes how people think or what they do, not merely because it can be packaged into a paper?
- Is there a reason this team can do something others are unlikely to do soon?
- Is the field young enough, confused enough, or misframed enough that a clear paper could shift the direction?
- Are you following a community default that might be wrong just because everyone copied it?

## Good signs

- The work clarifies a messy area or fixes a bad community habit.
- The best-case conclusion would sound meaningful even without the exact numbers.
- There is a concrete reason this project is not interchangeable with whatever any nearby group would publish next year.
- You can explain why this problem matters in one or two sentences without leaning on venue prestige.

## Warning signs

- The contribution is mainly "we also tried X".
- The project sounds like several smaller ideas bundled together because none feels strong enough alone.
- The argument for importance depends on a clicky title instead of a truthful conclusion.
- You are optimizing for "acceptable paper" rather than "important result".

## Useful prompts

- "If this succeeds wildly, what could the conclusion honestly say?"
- "What would make someone in the field change behavior after reading this?"
- "Why are we especially well placed to do this now?"
- "What would make this obviously unimportant even if the experiments look positive?"
