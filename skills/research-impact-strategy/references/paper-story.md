# Paper Story

Use this note when the research is real but the paper still does not land.

## One singular idea

- The paper should communicate one idea.
- Background, experiments, figures, and conclusion should all support that same idea.
- If the title is hard to write accurately, that often means the paper is trying to do too much.

## Know the reader

- Pick a concrete reader and write for that person.
- The introduction should meet the reader where they are, then move them into the paper's framing.
- If the reader is skeptical or unfamiliar with the setup, the introduction must do the positioning work before the method can matter.

## Title

- Accuracy matters more than cleverness.
- Do not use a broad or clicky title to imply a bigger contribution than the paper supports.
- Fix an overloaded paper before you try to "fix" the title.

## Introduction

- Treat the introduction as a story with a destination.
- State the world the reader currently believes in, then explain why your framing is the right one, then present the contribution.
- When the claim is controversial or non-obvious, order the evidence so the reader arrives at the conclusion rather than rejects it immediately.

## Figures

- Each figure should stand on its own and have one clear takeaway.
- If a caption cannot explain the point in one sentence, the figure is probably doing too much.
- Use figures to make the paper skimmable without losing the main result.

## Conclusion

- The conclusion is not the abstract in past tense.
- It should answer "so what?" directly and with some force.
- If the conclusion cannot say more than "the number improved a bit", the project may not be strategically strong enough.
