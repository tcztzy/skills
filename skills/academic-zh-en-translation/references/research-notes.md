# Research Notes For `academic-zh-en-translation`

This file records the source-backed guidance used to shape the skill.

## Source-backed rules

### 1. Completeness comes first
- The [EASE Guidelines for Authors and Translators of Scientific Articles to be Published in English](https://www.ease.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/doi.10.20316.ESE_.2018.44.e1.pdf) explicitly frame good scientific communication as `complete, concise, and clear`, and state that no necessary information should be missing.
- [GB/T 7713.2-2022](https://lib.tsinghua.edu.cn/wj/GBT7713_2-2022.pdf) says abstracts should be short statements without commentary, should be independent and self-contained, and that Chinese and foreign-language abstracts should correspond. It also notes that, for international exchange, the foreign-language abstract may contain more information.
- The [Tsinghua University graduate thesis writing guide](http://www.dhs.tsinghua.edu.cn/wp-content/uploads/2023/12/2024031107044595.pdf) and the [Peking University graduate thesis writing guide](https://grs.pku.edu.cn/docs/2018-03/20180301083100898652.pdf) both require the English abstract content to correspond to the Chinese abstract.

Working rule:
- Default translation mode means full correspondence and no omissions.
- If the user wants a more internationally adapted abstract that adds clarifying context, treat it as adaptation, not as plain translation.

### 2. English should be clear, not literal
- EASE says that, when translating, sentence structure should be modified if necessary to convey the message correctly or more clearly.
- EASE also recommends common, clear words over inflated wording, warns against excessive passive constructions, and gives a dedicated appendix on simplicity and cohesion.
- The [Academic Phrasebank](https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/) explains that research articles and dissertations rely on conventional rhetorical moves and reusable phraseology. Its section on [Describing methods](https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/describing-methods/) also notes that method descriptions are often written in the simple past tense, while [Discussing findings](https://www.phrasebank.manchester.ac.uk/discussing-findings/) emphasizes cautious language in discussion sections.
- The journal note [中英文摘要写作规范](http://xb.lhvtc.edu.cn/info/1019/1213.htm) says the English abstract should stay consistent with the Chinese content, remain concise, remove unnecessary fillers such as `It is reported`, `The author discusses`, and `In this paper`, prefer short sentences, use past tense for the author's work, use present tense for conclusions, and favor active voice where possible.

Working rule:
- Chinese-to-English translation should preserve content but freely recast syntax, information order, and clause structure where needed for idiomatic academic English.

### 3. Chinese should be rigorous, not Euro-Chinese
- GB/T 7713.2-2022 says the main text should be scientific, objective, accurate, complete, clear in hierarchy, logically tight, and smooth in wording.
- The Peking University guide says academic prose should be rigorous and concise, avoid literary or emotional language, and that abstracts should be independent, self-contained, and not written as a chapter-by-chapter outline.
- The journal note `中英文摘要写作规范` says humanities and social-science abstracts should use objective third-person narration and should avoid subjective formulas such as `作者认为` and `本文认为`, as well as mechanical formulas such as `通过……的研究` and `得出……的结论`.

Working rule:
- English-to-Chinese translation should aim for concise, objective, Chinese-native academic phrasing rather than preserving English word order.

### 4. Terminology discipline is mandatory
- EASE requires clear definition of abbreviations at first use and warns against using too many abbreviations.
- GB/T 7713.2-2022 says non-public abbreviations and terms in abstracts should be explained at first appearance.
- The Tsinghua and Peking University guides both stress consistency in document structure, keywords, captions, and naming.

Working rule:
- Build and maintain a terminology sheet before translating long or multi-section documents.

### 5. Structural correspondence matters in theses
- The Tsinghua guide treats Chinese abstract, English abstract, keywords, headings, and captions as distinct structural elements, and requires English keywords to correspond to the Chinese keywords.
- The Peking University guide does the same, and also requires the English abstract content to match the Chinese abstract.

Working rule:
- Do not treat headings, keywords, figure captions, appendix labels, or acknowledgements as optional extras.

## Where inference is involved
- The recommendation to split long Chinese sentences into shorter English clauses is an inference from EASE's clarity and simplicity guidance plus the journal note's advice on short English sentences.
- The recommendation to reorder dense English modifier chains into more natural Chinese topic-comment phrasing is an inference from GB/T 7713.2-2022, the Peking University guide, and the general contrast between English syntactic compression and Chinese readability.
- The recommendation to maintain hedging exactly in both directions is an inference from EASE and Academic Phrasebank discussion guidance, not a single quoted line.
