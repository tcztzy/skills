# Submission Readiness

Use this note when a paper is close to journal submission or is being prepared for resubmission after rejection.

## Audience and fit
- State who the journal's readers are and why the work matters to them.
- Frame the contribution in the landscape of the field, not as a prestige-journal imitation.
- If the work is a better fit for a narrower audience, say so plainly instead of forcing a broad-appeal framing.

## Title and abstract
- Keep both succinct, concrete, and proportionate to the evidence.
- Avoid hype words, inflated novelty claims, and sweeping promises the paper does not cash out.
- Make the main finding legible to a busy editor from the title and abstract alone.

## Closest prior work
- Compare against the most relevant prior papers prominently and fairly.
- Do not bury key comparisons in the appendix or cite only broad, flattering background.
- If the advance is incremental, describe it honestly and explain why the increment still matters.

## Cover letter
- Use the cover letter to explain fit, audience, and broader context in a few direct paragraphs.
- Do not paste a long list of unrelated high-profile papers to imply importance.
- If there is a special handling note, make it explicit and easy for the editor to verify.

## Figures and captions
- Assume many editors and reviewers will skim figures and captions before reading closely.
- Put major figures near their first substantive discussion when the format allows it.
- Make each caption describe what is shown and what the reader should take away.
- Label simulations, calculations, and experiments accurately instead of letting the prose blur them together.

## Resubmission and transfer hygiene
- Never reuse a cover letter with the wrong journal name.
- Do not resubmit a near-duplicate manuscript with only cosmetic title changes unless the venue explicitly requested revision.
- If the paper changed materially after prior reviews, say what changed and why it addresses the earlier concerns.
- Recommend journal transfers only when the destination is genuinely within scope.

## Editor and reviewer suggestions
- Suggest only technically relevant and reasonably unbiased reviewers.
- Avoid obvious conflicts such as recent collaborators, close colleagues, or people with a direct stake in the outcome.
- If the venue asks for editor or associate-editor suggestions, choose people whose scope actually matches the manuscript.
